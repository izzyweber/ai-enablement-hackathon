# A Hackathon-Powered Approach to AI Enablement

This playbook describes a hackathon format I'd run to launch company-wide AI enablement. To gain traction, new technologies don't need associated trainings as much as they require  practicality and fun. A hackathon does that for AI.

The structure has three phases. Preparation takes about a month, and the hackathon itself runs over about six weeks. A sustained programme begins the week after demo day and is designed to harness the excitement and ideas generated during the hackathon.

## Phase 1. Preparation

Leadership, IT, and I (your new AI enabler!) work together to produce three things:
1. A whitelist, which teams will operate under
2. A process for moving ideas from raw to deployed
3. A worked example that makes items 1 & 2 concrete

Only the whitelist is described here. The process and worked example are introduced at the hackathon kickoff.
### The AI data and tools whitelist

Teams brainstorm more freely when they already know the boundaries, and it's important to be clear about AI boundaries upfront, while allowing space to experiment. Writer's 2026 enterprise AI survey found two thirds of employees already use AI tools without IT approval. The whitelist closes that gap without killing momentum, and it gives IT peace of mind as they can configure tools properly before the hackathon and further AI enablement kicks off. 

#### Data categories

- **Red zone** (off-limits). Personal identifiable information, client confidentials, regulated data, sensitive performance tied to individual names
- **Yellow zone** (requires approval). Anonymised data, internal documentation, public research
- **Green zone** (minimal friction). Employees' own analysis, publicly available information, aggregated insights

#### Tool evaluation criteria

- Data retention and SOC 2 certification 
- API control vs web interface
- Role-based access and pricing that scales
- Usable by non-technical staff

#### Tool access (not just approval)

Teams should hit zero starting friction on day one of the hackathon. Tool licences need to be procured, accounts provisioned, and data paths to green and yellow zones established with clear permissions. 

#### Leadership and manager alignment

The preparation stage is the time to align leadership and team manager hackathon roles and buy-in. Ideally, leadership observes at ideation day without making decisions, then decides the winning team at demo day with a pre-agreed prize. Team managers are briefed on the whitelist and process so they aren't surprised when their reports pitch, and they co-host the build-sprint check-ins with me once projects are underway. 

## Phase 2. The hackathon!

### Kickoff

This session gives teams everything they need to start thinking seriously about what to pitch.

The IT team and I will walk teams through the data categories and the approved tools as well as boundaries for AI use. After the policy piece, I'll run short mini-sessions on each whitelisted tool so teams can see them in action. These work like tasting menus rather than training sessions. The point is for someone to walk out thinking "I didn't know that existed, and I can see three things in my week that it would change."
#### A worked example and suggested process

I walk teams through one complete pilot, from start to finish using a simple workflow: Brainstorm → AI draft (with guardrails) → Human review → Refinement → Quality check → Deploy.  

Teams leave kickoff knowing the rules, what "done" looks like, and what to bring to ideation day.

### Ideation day

First, individuals give five-minute pitches. Teams then coalesce around the one or two projects that most interest them. Smaller teams take one project; teams of over five people can run two projects.

Leadership sits in as observers, not deciders. They see the range of ideas and the energy. I would score transparently on impact, feasibility, capacity, and alignment, but for shortlisting rather than ranking.

### Feasibility vetting

Between ideation and build, I would run each shortlisted idea through a short check. Can we access the data safely? Does the tool exist or do we need to build something custom? Does IT agree? Do other teams need to partner? The output is a green light or a specific blocker.

### Build sprint

The build sprint runs three to four weeks. I hold weekly check-ins with each team, co-hosted by the team's manager so the project stays connected to real team priorities. I mentor teams, facilitate cross-team collaboration when ideas cross boundaries, and measure what changes.

Teams capture a baseline before the build starts. Without a measured pre-state, they can't make impact claims at demo day. 

### Demo day

Teams present what they built and what they learned. Leadership attends, picks a winning team, and awards a prize.

The prize signals that the organisation will back ideas that prove themselves. Protected build time for the winning team to extend their pilot works well, as does a named executive sponsor who champions the project over the next six months. 

## Phase 3. The sustained programme

The hackathon acts as a catalyst and generates three things the sustained work lives on:
1. Pilots needing adoption support
2. A pool of visible champions
3. Leadership managers, and employees with appreciation for the possibilities of AI

I'd plan to maintain a capability roadmap that sequences the next waves of use cases against organisational priorities. I'd also build learning pathways differentiated by seniority, with literacy for all staff, fluency for power users, and governance and strategy for managers and executives. The whitelist stays a living document, reviewed with IT as tools prove or disprove themselves in production. 

### Community of champions

The strongest contributors from demo day form the first champion cohort. We run a working Slack channel, monthly office hours, and a quarterly showcase that doubles as the pipeline for the next round of hackathon ideas. The time commitment stays light, at thirty to sixty minutes a week per champion, embedded in their normal job.

### Measurement cadence

I'd ask the following questions to measure success.

- Are the pilots still being used? 
- How do people feel about the work? I would run a one-question sentiment pulse quarterly, such as "Is AI making your job better, worse, or the same?" 
- What changed that we can measure? I compare baseline versus current state on each pilot's headline metric. This is grounded in pre-state from the hackathon team preparation.

## Why the hackathon-powered approach works

### Data safety and compliance comes first 

Writer's 2026 enterprise AI survey found 68% of employees use AI tools without IT approval, and 67% of executives believe their organisation has already leaked data through unapproved AI. The whitelist removes the ambiguity that drives that shadow use, without the friction of case-by-case review.

### Peer demonstration beats top-down training

McKinsey's 2026 Trust data shows knowledge and training gaps are now the top barrier to Responsible AI implementation, cited by 60% of respondents. The organisations scaling AI fastest credit peer-to-peer demonstration over classroom training. Citi scaled to over 4,000 AI Accelerators and hit more than 70% adoption; PwC Netherlands went from 300 enthusiasts to 6,000 employees in roughly a year. A hackathon produces those demonstrations at scale.

### Leadership ownership is built in

BCG's 2026 AI Radar found 72% of CEOs are now the primary AI decision-maker, double the 2025 figure, and 50% believe their job depends on getting AI right. Leadership observing at ideation day and owning the prize at demo day makes that ownership concrete.

### The programme doesn't end with the event

The hackathon catalyses, but the sustained programme is where adoption actually compounds. Champions recruit champions, the capability roadmap adapts to what the organisation needs next, and the measurement cadence keeps the work honest. Each cycle feeds the next, and I document as we scale so new teams don't start from zero.
